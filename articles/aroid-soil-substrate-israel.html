The Aroid Soil Mix Guide for Israel

By Pink Leaf Botanical Studios Updated April 2026 9 min read

More rare aroids die from the wrong soil than from any other cause — and in Israel, where indoor conditions swing between 30°C summer heat and AC-dried winter air, the margin for error is even thinner than in temperate climates. This guide covers what actually works for Monstera, Philodendron, Alocasia, and Anthurium substrates in Israeli conditions, which ingredients are available locally, and exactly what ratios to mix.

Why Substrate Is the Foundation of Aroid Health

Aroids — the family that includes Monstera, Philodendron, Alocasia, Anthurium, Pothos, and ZZ plants — are overwhelmingly epiphytes or hemi-epiphytes in the wild. In their native rainforests, they climb tree trunks with aerial roots that anchor into bark, moss, and leaf litter. Their root systems evolved for an environment with abundant moisture, constant airflow, and almost no compact soil.

When you plant an aroid in dense, water-retentive soil, you are asking roots adapted to bark and air to function in conditions closer to a swamp. Roots that cannot breathe rot. Roots that cannot grip decompose. The plant's first response is slowed growth; the second is yellowing leaves; the third is irreversible root collapse. By the time you see the symptoms, the damage is usually weeks old.

A correct substrate is not a minor factor — it is the single biggest variable under your control. Light and watering can be adjusted. Soil problems require full repotting, which is stressful to the plant and difficult once roots are already declining.

Why Standard Potting Soil Fails in Israel

The generic potting mixes sold in Israeli garden centres and supermarkets are blended for outdoor Mediterranean garden plants — geraniums, basil, succulents, annuals. Their composition is usually 60–70% peat, sphagnum moss, or composted bark dust, with a small amount of perlite added. This works for plants that want consistent moisture and accept some compaction.

For aroids in Israeli indoor conditions, this combination fails in three distinct ways:

  1. Compaction. Peat-heavy soil compresses over 4–6 months of watering, squeezing air pockets out of the root zone. Aerial roots suffocate. This happens faster in Israel than in cooler climates because evaporation rates push soil through more wet-dry cycles per week.
  2. Hydrophobic drying. Peat that fully dries out becomes hydrophobic — water runs off the surface without soaking in. Israeli summers produce exactly this result when collectors forget or delay watering during a heatwave. The next watering wets only the top layer, leaving roots in a dry pocket.
  3. Fungus gnat breeding. Moist, organic-rich soil is ideal fungus gnat habitat. In Israel, gnats spread across collections in days rather than weeks. The larvae graze on fine roots and stress the plant.
Imported aroids arrive in the wrong soil Rare plants imported from Europe or Asia are almost always potted in peat-heavy commercial mix for transport. The moment they arrive in an Israeli home, they begin the wet-dry cycle described above. Always plan to repot imported aroids into a proper chunky mix within 2–4 weeks of arrival — once they have acclimatised but before the transport substrate becomes a problem.

The Core Components of a Good Aroid Mix

A proper aroid substrate is coarse, porous, and drains freely. It is built from a small number of ingredients combined in ratios that match the plant and the environment. Here are the components worth knowing and using.

Orchid Bark (קליפת אורן)

Pine or fir bark in chunks of 8–15 mm is the single most important ingredient in a chunky aroid mix. It provides structure, resists compaction for years, and holds a moderate amount of moisture without becoming waterlogged. Aerial roots grip bark naturally, mimicking their wild behaviour on tree trunks.

In Israel, orchid bark is sold as "tree bark" (קליפת עץ) or "orchid bark" (קליפה לסחלב). Medium grade (8–15 mm) suits general mixes; small grade (5–8 mm) for seedlings and small pots; large grade (15–25 mm) for mature climbing aroids. Expect ₪30–₪50 per 5L bag.

Perlite (פרלייט)

Perlite is expanded volcanic glass — lightweight, sterile, non-decomposing, and permanent within the mix. It creates air pockets, prevents compaction, and allows water to drain quickly. A well-made chunky aroid mix contains 25–40% perlite by volume.

Use coarse-grade perlite (the large, chunky kind), not the fine horticultural grade sold for seed starting. Fine perlite compacts and defeats its own purpose. A 10L bag of coarse perlite runs ₪35–₪60 at Israeli garden centres; online bulk is often cheaper.

Coco Coir (קוקוס)

Coco coir is the ground-up husk of coconuts — a renewable, neutral-pH alternative to peat moss. Unlike peat, coir rehydrates easily after drying out (no hydrophobic problem), holds moisture without compacting as severely, and decomposes much more slowly. Coir provides the water-retentive element that balances bark and perlite.

Coco coir is sold in compressed bricks that expand enormously when soaked — one 650g brick makes about 8–10L of loose coir. Available at most Israeli garden centres and widely on AliExpress in bulk.

Pumice or Lava Rock

Pumice is porous volcanic rock with excellent water-holding capacity combined with perfect drainage. It is the premium alternative to perlite for high-value specimens. More durable (perlite will gradually break down after 2–3 years), heavier (roots grip it more firmly), and aesthetically appealing for collectors. The downside: harder to source in Israel. Most collectors order from AliExpress or Amazon Global.

Worm Castings (קומפוסט תולעים)

Worm castings are the nutrient-rich element of the mix. A small percentage (5–10%) provides a slow, sustained nitrogen source and populates the substrate with beneficial microbes. Do not overdo it — worm castings are rich, and too much will make the mix compact and waterlogged. Several organic farms in Israel produce and sell worm castings online.

Activated Charcoal (Optional)

Horticultural charcoal absorbs excess salts and impurities, helps buffer pH, and improves drainage. A small amount (roughly 5%) is beneficial but not essential for most rare aroids. Available through aquarium suppliers in Israel (used for filter media).

Three Reliable Recipes

These three recipes cover the full range of rare aroids commonly kept in Israel. Measure by volume, not weight — "parts" means equal loose-volume scoops.

Recipe 1: General Aroid Mix

For: Monstera deliciosa, Philodendron hederaceum, Pothos, Syngonium, most Alocasia, general-purpose use.

Medium orchid bark4Structure, drainage, root grip
Coarse perlite3Aeration, permanent drainage
Coco coir2Moisture retention without compaction
Worm castings1Nutrients, microbes

This is the default Pink Leaf mix — balanced for the range of aroids most Israeli collectors grow, forgiving of minor watering inconsistencies, and sustainable for 12–18 months before repotting is needed.

Recipe 2: High-Drainage Mix

For: Anthurium crystallinum, clarinervium, warocqueanum; variegated Alocasia; velvety Philodendron like gloriosum and verrucosum; rare high-humidity specimens.

IngredientPartsRole
Medium orchid bark4Primary structure
Coarse perlite or pumice4Maximum aeration
Sphagnum moss1Humid microclimate around roots
Charcoal1Filters salts, prevents stagnation

Almost no fine particles. This mix feels more like bark chunks than soil. It provides the constant air movement that epiphytic velvet aroids require, while sphagnum around the roots keeps humidity elevated in the immediate root zone.

Recipe 3: Seedling and Small-Pot Mix

For: newly propagated cuttings, small (10–12 cm) pots, young plants with fine roots.

Fine orchid bark (5–8 mm)3Smaller structure for finer roots
Perlite3Aeration

Mix dry, water the pot — not the pile Mix ingredients dry in a bucket or tub. Never pre-moisten a chunky aroid mix — wet bark clumps and wet perlite floats, making it impossible to blend evenly. After potting the plant, water the pot thoroughly to settle the substrate into place.

Species-Specific Notes

Monstera deliciosa and adansonii

The most forgiving of rare aroids. Recipe 1 works perfectly. Monstera tolerates slightly more moisture than most epiphytes, so do not go overboard reducing coir. Repot every 12–15 months — Monstera roots expand aggressively and will break terracotta pots if left too long.

Philodendron (general)

Climbing types (hederaceum, Brasil, micans) do well in Recipe 1. Crawling velvet types (gloriosum, verrucosum, plowmanii) need Recipe 2 — their rhizomes rot quickly in anything too moist. Do not bury the rhizome; it should rest on the substrate surface with roots descending from the nodes.

Alocasia

Standard species do well in Recipe 1. Variegated forms (Frydek variegata, Black Velvet variegated, Dragon Scale variegated) are among the most difficult aroids to keep in Israel and absolutely require Recipe 2. They hate wet roots combined with high heat — the most common failure mode in Israeli collections.

Anthurium

All collector anthuriums — crystallinum, clarinervium, warocqueanum, magnificum — need Recipe 2. Their fine aerial root systems rot within days in compact soil. Many advanced Anthurium growers skip soil entirely and grow in pure sphagnum moss or semi-hydroponic LECA. For beginners, Recipe 2 in a shallow terracotta pot is the easiest reliable method.

Hoya

Hoyas are epiphytes that want even sharper drainage than most aroids — consider adding 20% extra perlite to Recipe 1 and watering even less frequently. Hoyas will flower more reliably when slightly root-bound in a small pot with excellent drainage.

Repotting Timing and Technique

When to repot: Every 12–18 months, or when you see roots circling the bottom of the pot, emerging from drainage holes, or the soil has clearly broken down into dense compacted material. Spring (March–May) is ideal in Israel — the plant is entering its active growing season and will recover within weeks.

Pot size: Increase pot diameter by only 2–4 cm at a time. Too much extra soil around the root ball holds too much moisture and causes rot. Aroids prefer slightly snug pots.

Material: Terracotta is best for humidity-demanding species and beginners — the porous walls allow the substrate to dry more evenly. Plastic retains more moisture and is fine for forgiving species like Monstera and pothos in air-conditioned rooms.

Process:

  1. Water the plant lightly 1–2 days before repotting. Stressed dry roots are more fragile.
  2. Mix your substrate in a bucket and have it ready before you start.
  3. Gently remove the plant from its old pot. Massage the root ball to loosen compacted old soil and shake away as much as possible without damaging fine roots.
  4. Inspect roots. Healthy roots are firm and white or pale brown. Cut away any soft, mushy, or blackened roots with clean scissors.
  5. Place a layer of fresh mix in the bottom of the new pot. Set the plant so the base of the stem sits 1–2 cm below the rim. Fill around the roots with your mix, tapping the pot gently to settle the substrate. Do not pack it down with force.
  6. Water thoroughly until water runs from the drainage holes. This settles the substrate and wets all zones.
  7. Place the repotted plant back in its normal spot. Do not fertilize for 3–4 weeks — fresh substrate and disturbed roots cannot handle concentrated feeding.
Signs of a successful repot Within 2–3 weeks, you should see new root growth at the edges of the root ball, and new leaf development resuming on schedule. If the plant drops leaves, goes limp, or stops growing for more than a month, the roots may have been damaged during repotting or the new substrate is inappropriate for the species.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use regular potting soil for aroids in Israel?

No. Standard potting soil retains too much water and compacts quickly, starving aroid roots of oxygen. In Israeli heat and with AC cycling indoor humidity, compacted soil alternates between soaking wet and bone dry — both lethal to the fine aerial roots that rare aroids depend on. Use a chunky mix with at least 40% perlite or orchid bark.

What is the best aroid mix ratio?

A reliable general-purpose recipe for Israel is 40% orchid bark, 30% perlite, 20% coco coir, and 10% worm castings. For Monstera and Philodendron this ratio provides excellent drainage while holding enough moisture between waterings. For Anthurium and variegated Alocasia, increase the bark and perlite share to 70–80% of the total.

Where can I buy aroid soil components in Israel?

Perlite and orchid bark are available at most garden centres (משתלה), including Pinat Hazafon and Habonim. Coco coir is sold in compressed blocks at garden centres and on AliExpress for bulk. Worm castings are produced by several Israeli organic farms and sold online. For pumice and charcoal, AliExpress and Amazon Global are the most practical routes.

How often should I repot rare aroids?

Repot rare aroids every 12–18 months, or when you see roots circling the bottom of the pot and emerging through drainage holes. Spring (March–May) is the best time in Israel — the plant is entering active growth and will recover quickly. Avoid repotting during the hottest weeks of July–August when heat stress can compound transplant shock.

Do I need to add fertilizer to the soil mix?

A light amount of slow-release fertilizer or worm castings mixed into the substrate is beneficial but not essential. Chunky aroid mixes are nutrient-poor by design, so regular liquid feeding during the growing season (April–October) is more effective for ongoing nutrition than heavily amending the mix itself.

Should I use sphagnum moss for Monstera?

Pure sphagnum moss is excellent for propagating aerial root cuttings and for certain Anthurium species, but it is not suitable as a long-term potting medium for Monstera or other climbing aroids. Sphagnum holds too much water and breaks down too fast to support a mature climbing aroid. Use it for propagation, not as a substrate.
